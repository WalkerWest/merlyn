The origin

Vaughan Merlyn and the IT maturity model

In the early 1990s, Vaughan Merlyn — a partner at Ernst & Young — developed a framework for understanding how businesses relate to their technology providers. The model made a deceptively simple observation: not all businesses want the same thing from IT, and not all IT providers can deliver what businesses actually need.

The model organized business technology demand into three levels. At Level 1, businesses need reliable, cost-effective infrastructure — the lights stay on, the network works, the data is backed up. At Level 2, they need technology that improves how they operate — smarter processes, better data, automation that frees people for higher-value work. At Level 3, they need technology that changes what is strategically possible — decision intelligence, competitive modeling, the kind of insight that shapes where the business goes next.

The uncomfortable truth the model revealed was not about the demand side — it was about the supply side. The market is oversaturated with providers who can deliver Level 1 services. Commodity IT. MSPs. Help desks. Backup vendors. There are thousands of them, margins are thin, and businesses are not well served because no one is differentiated.

At Level 2 and Level 3, the picture inverts entirely. Demand is high — most mid-sized businesses desperately need process intelligence and decision support — but the supply of providers who can actually deliver it at a price mid-market businesses can afford is almost nonexistent. The firms that do it well charge enterprise rates. The firms that charge mid-market rates can't actually do it.

"The most important gap in the technology market is not the technology gap — it is the delivery gap between what mid-sized businesses need and what they can actually get."

— paraphrased from the Merlyn IT maturity framework, Ernst & Young

The connection

How the model became the company

The Merlyn framework shaped how the founding team thought about the AI services market long before Merlyn AI Labs existed. When we looked at the AI consulting landscape, the same dynamic was playing out — only more dramatically. The gap between what mid-sized businesses needed and what they could actually access had grown wider, not narrower, as AI emerged as a competitive differentiator.

The three service levels at Merlyn AI Labs map directly to the three levels of the model. Managed Foundation is Level 1 — reliable, professionally managed IT and AI infrastructure. Process Intelligence is Level 2 — deploying AI agents and models to improve how a business operates. Financial Digital Twin is Level 3 — executive-grade decision intelligence that changes what a business can see and do strategically.

We named the company for the framework because the framework explains why the company needs to exist. The gap is real, it is large, and mid-sized businesses in communities like Evansville deserve access to the full range of it.
